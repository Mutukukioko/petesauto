Car Leakages


Have you noticed some leakage(s) underneath your car?

Whether new or old, your car might experience leakage(s). For new cars, the risk of leakage might be minimal since it is new and still intact, whereas old machines are prone to leakages due to wearing out of different components.

Note: Not all leakages signify faultiness in a car.

Spotting Leakages

In order to spot leakages before they cause damage, always do random checks on your car. If possible, do daily checkups. For instance, you can move your car a step behind and keenly look on the ground where it was previously parked. If you spot a leakage underneath your car, please confirm which liquid is leaking. If not sure, please consult an expert.

Possible Leakages

  • Engine Oil

    The color of the engine oil could be black or brown. This depends on how well your car is maintained. Engine oil leakage signifies some faultiness in the engine, and therefore the engine should be inspected to spot the source of the leakage.

    • Faulty Oil Filter

      Leakage from the Oil Filter gasket may occur due to it being loose or worn out. After identifying the cause of the Oil Filter gasket leakage, you can either tighten it or replace it if it's worn out. Replacement should be in accordance with the manufacturer specifications.


    • Oil Pump Gasket

      Sometimes, with the aid of bolts and gasket, an external pump can be attached to the engine block. The bolts and gasket may loosen or wear out with time and may need to be adjusted or replaced. Inadequate oil can damage the oil pump, leading to it wearing out sooner than expected.


    • Damaged Oil Pan Gasket

      This is the seal between the Oil Pan and the engine block. This seal wears out with time due to pressure, temperature changes, and vibrations. The bolts can also loosen, thus causing the leakage. This can be solved by replacing the worn-out Oil Pan Gasket with a new gasket or tightening the loose bolts.


    • Cover Gasket

      This is a rubber seal that prevents oil leakage between the valve train components from the rest of the engine and also keeps in the oil used for the lubrication of those components.


      Signs of a faulty Cover Gasket include:

      • Smell of burning oil.
      • Smoke from the bonnet when the engine is running.
      • Oil Pressure Warning indicator may appear on the dashboard.

  • Power Steering Fluid

    Noticed a reddish-like fluid under your car? Your car could be leaking power steering fluid. This might be caused by a faulty or worn-out steering rack or a ruptured pipe that channels the power steering fluid. You should get it checked out to be sure of the problem so it could be fixed.


  • Automatic Transmission Fluid

    Automatic Transmission and Power Steering fluids are alike. In some cases, the Automatic Transmission fluid happens to be the same fluid used for Power Steering. When you notice a reddish-like fluid spillage underneath your car, especially on the gearbox side or between the gearbox and the engine, probably, your car gearbox seals are worn out and need to be replaced.

  • Coolant

    Coolant or Antifreeze is usually reddish or green in color. Leaking coolant could be due to a ruptured pipe or a damaged radiator. It could also be a sign of a faulty or nonfunctional water pump. When you notice its leakage, please get your car checked to avoid further damages as the car could experience overheating problems.


  • Brake Fluid

    If in any case you suspect your car is leaking brake fluid, make regular checks around the master cylinder, on the brake linings and drums, and generally around the car wheels. Brake fluid leaking could put the life of the occupants of the car and that of other road user(s) at risk. It should be fixed as soon as possible.


  • Fuel Leakage

    Fuel leakage is extremely hazardous and expensive to fix. Dangerous in the sense that it can cause fire explosion since petrol is highly flammable. Leaking of fuel could be due to a damaged fuel tank, fuel filter, fuel lines, or broken gas cap.


  • Wiper Fluid Leakage

    The windscreen cleaner fluid might leak when its tank is damaged, or maybe the pipes could be having a problem.

  • Air Conditioner

    It is normal for your AC to leak water under your car, especially on a sun-scorching day when you turn on the AC to full steam. The car AC is actually designed in a manner that it allows water to drain from the car. It works by sucking humidity from the air which due to condensation, forms water and is later drained.


Conclusion

In conclusion, regular check-ups are important for your car. You can make it a norm to always check your car no matter where you are before driving off. This will help you not only to notice leakages early enough but also scratches and dents. In case of leakage, first confirm which fluid is leaking before thinking of fixing it. Some are hands-on while others need the intervention of a professional to fix them. It is advisable to fix any leakage as soon as possible to avert further damages.
